Le Corbusier

Pioneer of Modern Architecture

About Le Corbusier

Le Corbusier, born Charles-Édouard Jeanneret-Gris, was a pioneering Swiss-French architect whose work revolutionized modern architecture through his emphasis on functionality, simplicity, and the use of modern materials like concrete, steel, and glass. He advocated for designs that prioritized human needs and the efficiency of urban spaces, challenging traditional architectural styles. Notable for his involvement in the International Style, Le Corbusier's most iconic works, such as Villa Savoye and the Unite d'Habitation, reflect his vision of open floor plans, communal living, and integration with nature, reshaping the way cities and homes were designed in the 20th century.

Famous Work: Villa Savoye

Villa Savoye, built between 1928 and 1931 in Poissy, France, exemplifies Le Corbusier's "Five Points of Architecture." These principles include pilotis (supports), a flat roof terrace, an open floor plan, horizontal windows, and a free façade. The pilotis lift the building above the ground, while the flat roof serves as an additional outdoor space. The open floor plan promotes flexibility, and the horizontal windows allow for ample natural light and views. The free façade enables a design free from structural constraints, making Villa Savoye a key example of modernist architecture.

Materials Used

The villa is constructed using reinforced concrete and glass, which allowed for the open floor plan and large horizontal windows.

  • Reinforced Concrete: Primary structural material forming the pilotis (columns) and overall structure
  • White Plaster: Smooth white-painted surfaces that became Le Corbusier's signature aesthetic
  • Glass: Extensive horizontal ribbon windows to maximize natural light
  • Steel: Used for window frames and structural reinforcement
