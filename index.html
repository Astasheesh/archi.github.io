Adolf Loos

Pioneer of Modern Architecture

About Adolf Loos

ARCHIII Loos was an Austrian and Czechoslovakian architect who grew up in Germany. He studied at the Dresden University of Technology, but soon became a dropout. He was the leading critic of the use of decoration and style in architecture. He argued that it needed to be functional, which is still in his writing “Ornamentation and Crime.” Loos viewed the use of design and ornament as childish and backward. He frequently railed against the idea that buildings should be made visually pleasing in any way, which did not add to the function of the structure.

Famous Work: Looshaus

The Looshaus, designed by Adolf Loos and completed in 1912, is an iconic example of Viennese Modernism located in Michaelerplatz, Vienna, Austria. This revolutionary building challenged the ornate traditions of architecture at the time by embracing minimalism and functional design. Its facade is divided into two distinct sections: the lower portion, clad in polished marble, houses shops, while the upper portion features plain plaster surfaces and residential apartments.

Loos rejected decorative elements, believing that "ornament is a crime," which sparked criticism and controversy at its unveiling. Despite its polarizing reception, the Looshaus is now recognized as a masterpiece that significantly influenced modern architectural philosophy.

Materials Used

The structure's square windows are arranged in a grid pattern, and the entire building is clad in a stone veneer, emphasizing its volume and mass. Contrary to the austere exterior, the interior of the Looshaus showcases a remarkable richness and warmth. It is an intimate domestic space characterized by a lavish use of luxurious materials like marble, wood, silk, and brass.
